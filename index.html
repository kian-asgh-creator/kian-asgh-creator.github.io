About

Who am I?

Kian Asgharzadeh is a classically trained pianist who started his musical journey at the age of five. Music has been integral to his growth in life and continues to be so. He has performed with the RUHS Jazz Band, who have performed locally and nationally, representing California at the 2020 Loyola Jazz Festival in New Orleans. A previous and concurring student of David Nuñez, Kian has received a Bachelor's degree in Music from U.C. Berkeley where he trained with Betty Woo.

He has been recognized as a Cole Scholarship recipient and received an honorable mention at the George and Beverly August scholarship competition. Asgharzadeh has played in numerous recitals at the Music Box, the George Nakano Theater, Daniel Weil Recital Hall, Wu Performance Hall, Hertz Hall, and more.

In addition to classical performance, Kian is passionate about composition, creating solo piano music and multimedia collaborative works for movement and visual arts. When he is away from the keyboard, Kian is also a Latin dancer who trains with Salsamania Productions and Kathy Reyes Dance Company, performing locally and nationally at congresses and intercollegiate events.

Process & Philosophy

How I approach
the work

I

Energy

As musicians we work with directing, transferring, and exploring energy across gradients through time and space, from large material cycles in forms to small cycles in the form of lines and breaths. I find deep joy in tracking the micro-relationships between notes, segments, and functions within a musical form, and equal joy in breaking that form open to discover new energetic possibilities. At the macro level, I'm drawn to the relationship between two large bodies of art across different media. What happens when music and movement, or sound and image, occupy the same space and speak with each other?

II

Collaboration

Some of my most meaningful work lives at the boundary between music and other art forms - scored for movement, composed for film, performed alongside dance, text, or visual art. I believe how sound behaves, and how we listen to it, changes momentarily depending on context. In collaborative work, I listen for what to compose, treating the choreographer's vocabulary or the filmmaker's image as a living material rather than a static shape. Like a dance between partners, the musical body becomes a complement to the work it shares space and time with.

III

Approach

The way I live through music is built through years of classical training, improvisational movement, Latin rhythm, and contemporary harmony — I don't treat these monoliths of form and style as separate compartments All of the techniques, styles, and ways of being across music, movement, and life hold secret fundamentals and hints toward the true nature of what this is, and there is constant communication and overlap between seemingly opposititional ways of approaching sound and art. The work I make is an ongoing inquiry into what it means to hold all of that at once.
